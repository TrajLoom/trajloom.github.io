Pipeline Overview

Overview of TrajLoom. Given observed trajectories \( \mathcal{T}^{p} \), we rasterize and encode them with
Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding into a dense offset field, then compress with TrajLoom-VAE into
history latents \( \mathbf{z}^{p} \). Conditioned on \( \mathbf{z}^{p} \) and video features, TrajLoom-Flow generates future latents
via rectified-flow integration with boundary hints, which are decoded by TrajLoom-VAE into future trajectories \( \hat{\mathcal{T}}^{f} \).

1. Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding

Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding reduces location-dependent bias by representing each observed point as an offset from its pixel-center anchor, producing a dense motion field that is easier to model than absolute coordinates.

2. TrajLoom-VAE

TrajLoom-VAE learns a compact spatiotemporal latent space for dense trajectories with masked reconstruction and a spatiotemporal consistency regularizer.

3. TrajLoom-Flow

TrajLoom-Flow predicts the full future trajectory window in the latent space from history latents, visibility, and video features, using boundary cues and on-policy K-step fine-tuning for stable long-horizon sampling.

Abstract

Predicting future motion is crucial in video understanding and controllable video generation. Dense point trajectories are a compact, expressive motion representation, but modeling their future evolution from observed video remains challenging. We propose a framework that predicts future trajectories and visibility from past trajectories and video context. Our method has three components: (1) Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding, which reduces location-dependent bias by representing each point as an offset from its pixel-center anchor; (2) TrajLoom-VAE, which learns a compact spatiotemporal latent space for dense trajectories with masked reconstruction and a spatiotemporal consistency regularizer; and (3) TrajLoom-Flow, which generates future trajectories in latent space via flow matching, with boundary cues and on-policy K-step fine-tuning for stable sampling. We also introduce TrajLoomBench, a unified benchmark spanning real and synthetic videos with a standardized setup aligned with video-generation benchmarks. Compared with state-of-the-art methods, our approach extends the prediction horizon from 24 to 81 frames while improving motion realism and stability across datasets. The predicted trajectories directly support downstream video generation and editing.

1. Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding

Starting from sparse trajectories \( \mathcal{T}=\{\tau_n\}_{n=1}^{N} \), Grid-Anchor Offset Encoding constructs a dense trajectory representation. Instead of representing motion by absolute image coordinates, it represents each pixel by its displacement from a local pixel-center anchor. This produces a zero-centered, location-consistent representation that is easier to model across different image regions.

Why this helps?

With absolute coordinates, trajectory variance is strongly dominated by grid location: points from different cells are centered at different image positions even when their local motion is similar. By subtracting the pixel-center anchor, the encoding removes most of this location-driven bias and focuses the representation on local displacement. This makes the motion field more uniform and easier for the downstream VAE and flow model to learn.

2. TrajLoom-VAE

Dense trajectory fields are high-dimensional, so TrajLoom-VAE learns a compact spatiotemporal latent space for efficient future generation. A visibility-masked pointwise reconstruction loss encourages the reconstruction to match the target at visible locations, but it does not directly model temporal evolution or local relative motion. As a result, reconstruction error alone can still produce temporally jittery or locally inconsistent motion. To address this, we add a spatiotemporal consistency regularizer that matches both temporal velocities and multiscale spatial neighbor relations between the target segment and the reconstruction.

Why the regularizer is important?

The toy example above shows the core issue. A smooth reconstruction and a jittery reconstruction can have the same pointwise reconstruction loss, because both match the ground truth equally well at individual frames. However, they imply very different motion over time: the smooth reconstruction preserves consistent velocity, while the jittery one alternates its frame-to-frame displacement. The consistency regularizer assigns a low penalty to smooth motion and a high penalty to jitter, helping the latent space prefer temporally smooth and locally coherent trajectories.

3. TrajLoom-Flow

TrajLoom-Flow generates future trajectories in the latent space learned by TrajLoom-VAE. It is conditioned on historical latents, visibility, video features, and a text prompt, and learns a conditional velocity field to model future trajectory.

Boundary cues

We use two lightweight boundary cues to help predictions stay aligned with the observed past. First, the latent state is anchored with the last history latent slice as shown above, which encourages continuity at the history-future boundary. Second, we inject token-aligned history features directly into the generator.

On-policy K-step fine-tuning

A standard flow-matching objective trains the model on interpolated latent states, but sampling uses states produced by integrating the learned ODE. This train-test mismatch can cause drift over long horizons. To reduce that gap, we apply on-policy K-step fine-tuning, which lets the generator learn from its own rollout states and improves stability during sampling.
